GMS Week 29 – BLOCKADE RETURNS, TOLL RISES

Hellenic Shipping News  |  18 July 2026  |  https://www.hellenicshippingnews.com/gms-week-29-blockade-returns-toll-rises

The second act arrived with a name this time, and it was not “ceasefire.” Five consecutive days of American strikes through Wednesday hit Iranianpositions from Bandar Abbas and Qeshm Island to Sirik, while Washington reinstated the naval blockade of Iranian ports lifted during June’s interim peace. The blockade found its first target within a day. A Curaçao-flagged tanker bound for Kharg Island ignored repeated warnings and was disabled by a Hellfire strike into its smokestack, stopping the vessel without spilling its cargo. Two others were redirected without incident. President Trump is reportedly weighing a broader campaign and has revived the idea of seizing Kharg Island outright, while abandoning the proposed 20% Hormuz transit fee. Nobody involved is using the word peace this week, which may be the most accurate statement anyone has made since February.

Oil has now made this climb three times since the conflict opened, and the muscle memory shows. Brent touched USD 84.77 and WTI USD 79.56 on Thursday, one-month highs that recovered roughly one-third of the ground surrendered during June’s interim peace. A reinstated blockade and open discussion of taking a major export terminal will do that. Yet the move remains smaller than the first shock, and the market’s patience is noticeably shorter. Traders are repricing a war whose script they can now recite from memory.

Freight, characteristically, is having a different week. The Baltic Dry Index closed Friday at 2,944, its highest since June 5, while the Capesize Index rose 1.9% to 4,655 on continued iron ore and coal demand. Dry bulk owners are pricing cargo fundamentals rather than Hormuz, and for another week, cargo fundamentals are winning the argument. The recycling implication remains familiar. Firmer earnings keep ageing tonnage trading, while renewed war risk interrupts the Gulf exit queue.

The data released this week was arguing with the news that overtook it. U.S. June inflation eased to 3.5% from 4.2%, its first decline in five months, with core inflation slowing to 2.6% as the energy relief delivered by the now-defunct ceasefire reached the figures. India’s June print moved the other way, rising to 4.38% from 3.93%, its highest since December 2024 and above expectations as the spring energy shock reached transport and food costs. The calm print anticipated last week did not arrive, and the Rupee noticed. USD/INR touched 96.40 on Thursday before easing toward 96.20. The Taka held near 123.30, the Turkish Lira reached another record near 47.10, and the Pakistani Rupee barely moved, the only basin currency that appears to have opted out of 2026.

In Bangladesh, the numbers that matter most are not economic. Flooding and landslides across Chattogram, Cox’s Bazar, Habiganj, and neighbouring districts have killed at least 51 people and affected more than one million. A failed embankment on the Khowai River flooded twenty-five villages, while a landslide inside a Cox’s Bazar refugee camp killed sixteen Rohingya residents, including children. Against that human toll, disruption to Chattogram’s yards is secondary, but commercially real. Beaching has slowed to a near-halt for a second consecutive week, and the Gulf exit queue that was expected to test the port’s capacity this month now waits on a recovery that has not arrived.

The war has returned to the strait just as the weather has closed the beach. Firmer freight keeps candidates trading, the blockade delays the Gulf exit queue, and flooding prevents Chattogram from absorbing whatever reaches it. The market remains paused, not broken. The ships wait for passage. The yards wait for dry land.
