## Main
in Weekly Demolition Reports 30/03/2026
Image reference: 2026-03-30_gms-week-13-wait-goes-on_img1.jpg
As the world's ship recycling markets emerge from the Eid al-Fitr holidays, they do so into an environment thatcontinues to generate more questions than answers. The post-holiday reopening, always anticipated as a potential catalyst for pent-up activity, has instead delivered something more characteristic of the broader 2026 narrative: confusion. Steel plate prices played opposite roles in Pakistan and India this week, as Indian levels recorded an impressive jump while Pakistani levels continued to bleed, declining non-stop over the course of the last month now. Bangladeshi and Chinese levels continued to flatline at the bottom of the pool, offering little directional signal to a market already starved of pricing clarity.
Even the U.S. Dollar, which has otherwise been a reliable source of sub-continent pressure this year, chose this week to remain undecided, making firm improvements against some recycling currencies while losing ground against others. The mixed signals have left buyers and sellers alike struggling to anchor their negotiating positions, and the result has been another week in which market activity remained well below the levels needed to make meaningful inroads into the tonnage backlog that has been building throughout Q1.
Bangladesh has emerged from the Eid break in a surprisingly bullish frame of mind, likely on the back of a few hungry recyclers whose yards have cleared sufficient space to welcome fresh tonnage. This is a noteworthy shift from the cautious posture that defined the Chattogram market through the holiday period. India and Pakistan, by contrast, struggled to keep pace despite the end of the holy month, hampered by the persistent absence of workable tonnage and the ongoing uncertainty around steel economics that has made confident bidding difficult to sustain.
The broader macro context remains unchanged and unresolved. The Middle East conflict continues to keep freight earnings elevated, discouraging owners from sending ageing tonnage for recycling. The Baltic Dry Index has yet to give meaningful ground. Oil remains firmly above USD 100/barrel, reinforcing the perverse arithmetic of a market where higher energy prices translate into longer trading lives for the very vessels that ship recyclers need. As Week 13 closes and Q2 begins, the industry finds itself asking the same question it has asked for most of the past year: when will the tonnage arrive in sufficient quantity to match the appetite that recyclers, particularly in Bangladesh, are beginning to demonstrate?

Bangladesh enters the post-Eid period with an appetite that has caught more than a few observers

The two OFAC-sanctioned VLCCs that have been idling at OPL Chattogram since before the Eid

immediate horizon. The episode has sharpened due diligence practices among cash buyers and
recyclers, who are now increasingly vigilant in their vetting of proposed tonnage, particularly units
that may have changed hands through channels connected to sanctioned parties. Reports suggest
there are still a handful of similarly suspect units in cash buyer hands awaiting introduction to the
market. Given the fate of the Chattogram duo, how these pending and future dealings will

India delivered one of the more surprising data points of the week in the form of a sharp upward move
in local steel plate prices, an impressive jump that has provided Alang's recyclers with at least a S teel firms.

softness that has characterised Indian steel economics for much of Q1, though veterans of the Alang
market will be cautious about extrapolating too much from a single week's movement in a market that
has demonstrated a pronounced tendency to give back gains almost as quickly as it makes them.
Lacking tonnage.
The deeper structural challenge at Alang remains unchanged: there is simply not enough workable
tonnage arriving at the waterfront. Strong freight earnings, elevated oil prices, and owners' continued
preference for keeping aging vessels in service rather than sending them for recycling have combined
to leave Alang's anchorage thin and its yards underutilised. The arrival of a handful of smaller regional
general cargo vessels, largely units displaced from war-affected trading routes, has provided some
activity, but the larger LDT units that Alang's recyclers need to generate meaningful revenue remain
stubbornly absent from the demolition pipeline.
India's strongest competitive position continues to be its HKC compliance infrastructure. With more

continues to compress the margin available to Gadani's recyclers on each unit they acquire.
Buyers ready to acquire

and the relative availability of yard space has made Gadani a viable landing point for owners who
need a compliant, accessible South Asian alternative to Alang.

TURKEY
S
TEADY BUT SILENT!
Turkey ends Week 13 in much the same position it has occupied for the better part of the year:
structurally disadvantaged on price, watching the sub-continent capture what little tonnage is
available, and waiting for a market shift that shows no sign of arriving on the immediate horizon.
Disadvantaged.
Aliaga's yards continued to operate at subdued levels through the post-Eid week, with no significant
sales or major new enquiries reported across the period.
The Turkish Lira's continuing depreciation against the U.S. Dollar remains the defining constraint on
Turkish recycling economics. At indicative levels of USD 265-285/LDT depending on vessel type,
Aliaga cannot realistically compete with sub-continent pricing for mainstream commercial tonnage.
The domestic steel scrap market, which ultimately sets the ceiling on what Turkish recyclers can
offer, continues to reflect sluggish demand from the construction sector and the broader uncertainty
that has characterised the Turkish economic environment through Q1.
Turkey's structural advantage in the specialist segment, EU-approved yards able to receive EU-
flagged vessels under the Ship Recycling Regulation, continues to provide a thin but durable revenue
stream for the handful of facilities that hold approved status. The Ege Çelik delisting and the
reputational overhang it created for Aliaga's regulatory credibility has not helped, and European
owners considering Turkish yards for their regulated tonnage will continue to apply careful scrutiny.
For the broader Turkish market, the question of when it next becomes genuinely competitive for
international recycling tonnage remains open, and the answer, for now, does not appear to be this
week.
