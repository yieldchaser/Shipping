Highlights of the Week
We'd like to start out by wishing our readers a "happy new year". Although no one can predict the future, it is helpful to start a new year with an understanding of the main
concerns that will probably dominate the global agenda. 2023 won't be a monotonous year, a fact that is already evident.
In 2023, Asia will see challenging economic conditions. After several years of rapid export development, the region will experience a decline as the US economy is predicted to
contract significantly and the EU will go into recession. The outlook for Asia's domestic demand is also uncertain as local economies adjust to the increased interest rate
implemented in 2022 to combat inflation. The likelihood of North Korea restarting its nuclear tests and Taiwan's election preparations will add to the geopolitical risk.
Southeast Asia's increased political instability will give India the chance to attract more attention from international manufacturers. The Indian government has its own political
concerns, but from the perspective of investors, it also administers consistent policy and reduces the possibility of a change in leadership.
In 2022, supply chain disruptions caused problems in both the EU and the US due to ongoing problems with the gas and energy supply chains, this may persist across
European nations in the following year. However, the current Infrastructure Law is expected to increase infrastructure investments, which will likely cause the US economy to
gain momentum, particularly in the automotive and infrastructure sectors.
Regarding the steel industry In 2023, it's anticipated that the US steel market will stay stable. Turkey and other Central and South American nations are all scaling back their
construction projects as a result of inflation and interest rates, and in these locations, the steel industry is expected to experience a decline in 2023.
China is currently experiencing a wave of coronavirus infections, yet Beijing continues to move on with its plans to relax its regulations. In a significant move toward reopening
its borders, it declared this week that international arrivals will no longer be subject to quarantine restrictions beginning in early January.
In October, the International Monetary Fund predicted that in 2023, the rate of global growth will drop to 2.7%. The group issued a warning in November that since issuing that
forecast, the picture had grown even "gloomier." The actions of central banks going forward, the effects of China's impending reopening, and energy prices may determine if a
worldwide recession occurs.
