Highlights of the Week
Global steel prices are experiencing a downward pull due to plummeting demand from the end-user industries. Despite the ongoing volatility in steel
prices, ship recyclers from all major recycling destinations are maintaining stable offer prices due to non-availability of tonnage in the recycling
market. However, the end buyers are more inclined towards acquiring smaller tonnage as the rising inflation, economic slowdown, and depleting
forex reserves have lowered their purchasing power and risk appetite.
Iron ore extended its rout to the lowest level in more than two years on mounting concerns over global steel demand. The steel-making ingredient
hit USD 82.45/MT in Singapore on Thursday, its lowest since May 2020. Iron ore has been under pressure as consumption weakens in China because
of the nation's housing market woes and Covid restrictions. Demand worldwide is also fading as tighter monetary policy and Europe's energy crisis
roils major economies.
Steel demand in the European Union is expected to erode by 3.5% this year and fall 1.9% in 2023, hit by soaring energy prices and a looming
recession. This is the second time this year the European Steel Association has downgraded its forecast for apparent steel demand, which measures
output of steel producers plus net imports minus net exports.
The International Monetary Fund (IMF) has downgraded its economic outlook for Asia as global monetary tightening, rising inflation blamed on the
war in Ukraine, and China's sharp slowdown dampen the region's recovery prospects. Among the biggest headwinds is China's rapid and broad-
based economic slowdown blamed on strict COVID-19 lockdowns and its worsening property woes, the IMF said.
