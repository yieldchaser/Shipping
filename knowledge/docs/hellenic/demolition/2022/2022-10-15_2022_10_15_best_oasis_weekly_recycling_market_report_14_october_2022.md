Highlights of the Week
Steel prices worldwide are under pressure due to the alarm bells ringing for a possible recessionary phase in most regions. In the ship recycling
industry, even though the major recycling destinations are facing issues such as fluctuating currency exchange rates, restrictions on Letters of Credit
issuance and dwindling demand, offer prices remain stable week-on-week due to the shortage of tonnage available for recycling. Among the
subcontinent players, Bangladesh is leading the price board, closely followed by India and Pakistan.
European countries have begun implementing emergency measures to mitigate rising energy prices. The steel sector is among those energy-
intensive industries that are particularly vulnerable to the ongoing energy crisis, thus impacting steel prices. Rising input costs, on top of eroding
demand, have already caused a wave of curtailments that threaten the viability of Europe's entire metal sector.
A significant consumer pullback is showing up in ocean shipping, with almost a 20% drop in ocean freight orders for the months of September and
October. The decline in demand cuts across many products, resulting due to a combination of too much inventory coupled with a lack of clarity on
consumer demand.
The International Monetary Fund has cut its growth forecast for 2023 and warned of a cost of living crisis as the global economy continues to be
affected by the war in Ukraine, broadening inflation pressures and a slowdown in China. The fund maintained its global economic estimate for this
year at 3.2 per cent but downgraded next year's forecast to 2.7 per cent. In short, the worst is yet to come, and for many people 2023 will feel like a
recession," said Pierre-Olivier Gourinchas, IMF chief economist.
