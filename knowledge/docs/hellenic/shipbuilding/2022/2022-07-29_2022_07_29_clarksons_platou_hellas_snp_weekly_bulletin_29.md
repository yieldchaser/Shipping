New Building
In tankers this week, it has been reported that Pacific Carriers ordered two 50k dwt MR's at K Shipbuilding, with both vessels set to deliver
within 2024. Furetank Rederi announced that they had ordered a firm 18k dwt Chemical tanker at CMJL Yangzhou, extending their series to
eleven at the yard. The vessel will be equipped with LNG dual-fuel propulsion and Ice Class 1A.
In dry bulk, U-Ming Marine announced orders for two firm 64k Ultramax at New Dayang Shipyard, with the vessels expected to deliver within
the second half of 2024.
In the gas carrier market, Daewoo (DSME) announced that they had taken orders for two firm 174k CBM LNG Carriers from an unknown
owner (reportedly associates of ADS Maritime), with the vessels both slated for delivery in the second half of 2026.
In containers, just the one order this week as Haidong shipyard announced contracting a firm gearless 2,206 TEU containership for their
domestic counterpart Xiamen Dingxiangshun Shipping, with the vessel set to deliver in 2024.
Recylcing
Catch 22!
There was more upheaval to report this week, this time coming from Bangladesh, where despite price levels showing signs of improving, the
Bangladeshi Government introduced new restrictions to limit the outgoing volume of U.S. Dollars for ‘essential' purchases only. This will now
pose serious questions concerning these improved levels for any available tonnage in the foreseeable future. As such, the government
announced that any Letter of Credit exceeding USD 5.0 million must be approved by the Central State Bank for opening. In addition, reports
this week suggest that the Government of Bangladesh has sought a USD 4.5 billion loan from the International Monetary Fund as it seeks
assistance to cope with the mounting pressure on their economy. It is understood the Bangladeshi Taka has devalued by about 20 pct
against the U.S. Dollar over the last few months weakening the country's finances.
We are already hearing of cash buyers facing difficulties reselling any larger LDT tonnage that they have in hand due to these restraints and
it is expected that this will continue for some time, thus the question will be, whether Bangladesh will be aggressive to purchase any large
LDT vessel that comes into the market. Concerning times indeed for the local Chattogram market!
Elsewhere, India is seemingly the more stable and competitive market, but again the dwindling of tonnage being circulated is a cause for
concern for the domestic industry. Across their border, the recyclers in Pakistan look set to take a position on the side-lines as their price levels
remain far less competitive than their counterparts in India and even Bangladesh.
