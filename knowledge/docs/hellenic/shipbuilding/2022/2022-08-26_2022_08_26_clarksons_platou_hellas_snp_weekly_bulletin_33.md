New Building
In tankers this week, MOL announced ordering two firm 309k dwt VLCC's at Dalian COSCO KHI (DACKs). The vessels are set
to be fitted with LNG dual-fuel propulsion and will deliver in 2025 and 2026 respectively.
In dry bulk, MOL announced contracting four firm LNG dual-fuel 210k dwt Newcastlemaxes at CSSC Beihai, with the first two
vessels to deliver within 2025 and the third and fourth vessels within 2026. Jiangmen Nanyang took orders two firm 40.5k dwt
Open Hatch Handysizes from an unknown domestic customer, with delivery expected in 2024.
In gas carriers, CSSC Hudong Zhonghua announced contracting a firm 14k CBM LNG Bunkering Vessel for Wuhu LNG, with
the vessel set to deliver in August 2024.
In containers, it was reported that Elbdeich Reederie ordered two firm 1,400 TEU Containerships at Penglai Jinglu. The vessels
are expected to deliver in 2024 and extends their series to six at the yard.
Recycling
Rebound!
There was more optimism this week as several cash buyers started to take a positive view that the market will rebound later
in the year, with the sale of a VLCC which achieved a premium price compared to recent numbers that have been on offer.
It is understood the actual delivery of the unit is for end October when many feel that the banking/USD issues Bangladesh is
currently facing will be resolved and the opening of Letters of Credit returning to the norm will once again be approved,
therefore potentially an aggressive appetite could return to the market locally.
There is also still a stark lack of available tonnage and any Owners wishing to test the waters this year will have been pleasantly
happy with the levels that they have achieved. Although the only time Owners have been tempted is when the vessels SS/DD
has been imminently due, making the costs often unfeasible and therefore recycling the most commercial option.
There have also been more discussions that the US-Iranian talks are getting closer to a conclusion. If a deal is restored, a deal
here could also allow the international fleet to operate the trade rather than the sub-defused fleet. A rough estimate is that
around 200 tankers are active in this fleet trade (covering Iran and Venezuela), whether these tankers move into the
sanctioned Russian trade or into the international fleet remains to be seen, if they do this will help push more vessels into the
recycling market.
